Monitoring the global capital flows over the past few days, the chasing frenzy triggered by geopolitical panic has noticeably cooled, replaced by a rational reassessment after intense long-short tug-of-wars.

At Friday's close, as global commodities and bond markets settled, Brent crude futures **dropped 5.5%** to $105.16 per barrel, while WTI crude broke below the $100 mark to close at $96.35 [Reuters]. The commodity long premium, previously driven entirely by Middle East tensions and expectations of a Strait of Hormuz blockade, faced significant profit-taking on the day diplomatic talks showed glimmers of hope [Reuters].

Many investors might view this as a brief retracement amid geopolitical uncertainties. However, turning our gaze to the Federal Reserve’s hidden cards reveals that the macroeconomic pressure on high-valuation assets has not truly eased.

### I. The Illusion of Peace: Oil Volatility and the Liquidity Logic Behind the Warsh Mindset
Many are focused on Friday's late-day surge in US equities, feeling optimistic as the Dow Jones Industrial Average crossed the 50,000 mark [Reuters]. The prevailing market expectation is that easing US-Iran tensions and plunging oil prices will directly alleviate inflationary crises.

Yet, this optimistic view may overlook deeper monetary policy signals.

The [FOMC April Minutes](https://wsj.com) released this week revealed that most officials expressed deep concerns over sticky inflation. More importantly, **an increasing number of members explicitly stated that they would consider further rate hikes if inflation persists**. The hawkish stance of the newly appointed Chairman, Kevin Warsh, implies that the market’s anticipated "rate-cut cycle" is unlikely to materialize anytime soon.

Consequently, while falling oil prices caused Treasury yields to pull back from their highs, the 10-year US Treasury yield remains pinned at a relatively high level of **4.60%**.

A 4.60% yield means that borrowing costs for the real economy and credit markets remain elevated. The US 30-year fixed mortgage rate has climbed to a seven-week high of **6.56%**. This indicates that "higher for longer" interest rates are a reality, and global macroeconomic liquidity remains in a net outflow state.

**【Transmission Chain of Macro Liquidity Tightening】**
Fed Hints at Hikes 📈 ➡️ Financing & Borrowing Costs Climb 🚀 ➡️ Credit Tightens in Real Economy 📉 ➡️ High-Valuation Risk Assets Under Pressure 🚨

On the fundamental side, the UK's May Composite PMI fell to **48.5**, entering contraction for the first time in a year, while private sectors in Germany and France continue to drag under the aftereffects of high energy costs. In contrast, though the US May Manufacturing PMI printed a high **55.3**, it was primarily driven by companies **front-loading orders defensively** out of fear of geopolitical logistics disruptions. This atypical prosperity looks more like a short-lived phenomenon in a supply chain restructuring phase.

### II. Divergence in Core Asset Pricing Reflects Genuine Risks
From a long-term perspective of macro liquidity and commodities, this week’s pricing of core assets showcases a sharp divergence in styles:

*   **Nvidia (NVDA)** — The AI anchor throwing out a staggering $91 billion revenue outlook [Reuters].
*   **Spot Gold (XAUUSD)** — The defensive line holding firm in high-level consolidation at $4,546/oz [Sina Finance].
*   **Dollar Index (DXY)** — A brief breathing window, easing slightly to 99.13.

On the surface, Nvidia led a powerful tech rebound at the end of the week with an unrivaled earnings outlook, seemingly immune to the Fed’s hawkish warnings [Reuters]. However, this can easily lead investors who overlook macro signals into a cognitive trap: believing that as long as AI leaders possess flawless fundamentals, asset valuations can permanently decouple from the interest rate environment.

In fact, the US Dollar Index holding stubbornly above 99 serves as a silent warning.

When systemic liquidity tightens globally, a localized earnings party cannot defy the gravity of the broader market. Nvidia's fundamentals remain strong and tech giants' capital expenditure has not paused, but these high valuations are built on the assumption of "rapid growth in distant future cash flows." With risk-free rates remaining elevated for the long haul, all growth stock valuations are facing a brutal discount rate stress test.

Meanwhile, COMEX Gold futures held firmly at **$4,546/oz** [Sina Finance], and Bitcoin fluctuated around the $80,500 high. This indicates that smart money is looking through long-term risks in fiat currency credit and sovereign debt, actively rotating into hard assets.

### III. The Mirror of History: Risks of Blind Optimism Under the Stagflation Shadow
Markets easily get comfortable with the smooth sailing of past low-interest-rate eras, treating short-term commodity fluctuations from geopolitical events as a persistent norm. However, historical cycles suggest that maintaining heavy long positions under this macro framework amounts to placing bets using the wrong analytical framework.

*   **The 1970s Stagflation Era**: Marginal shifts in Middle East tensions caused oil prices to repeatedly whip back and forth. The Fed continuously oscillated between "controlling inflation" and "supporting growth," causing investors without macro perspective to face severe capital losses during long-short transitions.
*   **The 2000 Dot-com Bubble**: Right before the tech crash, financial reports of select flagship stocks still exceeded expectations, and the market loudly proclaimed that "this time it's backed by real fundamentals." Yet, when global liquidity dried up, no single sector was completely immune.

Next week, the market faces the ultimate test with the Fed’s most watched **PCE inflation data**. As more explicit monetary policy details from the Warsh era emerge, and earnings from retail and tech giants expose how high interest rates bite into real consumer spending, market pricing may undergo a more substantial correction.

3.  **Rotate Toward Inflation-Resistant Hard Assets**: Under long-term high interest rates and credit cycle uncertainties, appropriately overweight assets like gold that possess anti-inflation and anti-sovereign credit risk attributes [Sina Finance]. This remains an effective hedging tool against macro uncertainty.
